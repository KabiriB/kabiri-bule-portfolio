Python Project: Human Development & Economic Patterns

Overview

This project explores the relationship between economic output and human development using country-level data on GDP per capita and the Human Development Index (HDI). The core question is simple but important: Do higher-income countries always deliver better human outcomes?

Using Python, I carried out an exploratory analysis to understand global patterns, regional clusters, and exceptions to the rule — countries that achieve relatively high human development despite modest income levels.

Analytical Approach

The analysis is structured as a clear, reproducible workflow inside a Jupyter Notebook:

  • Loading and cleaning HDI and GDP per capita data for multiple countries and regions.
  • Creating scatterplots to visualise the relationship between GDP and HDI, including log-scaling GDP for readability.
  • Highlighting regional groupings (such as Sub-Saharan Africa, Europe & Central Asia, East Asia & Pacific) to compare trajectories.
  • Identifying countries that “punch above their weight” on HDI relative to income.

The focus is not only on correlation, but on the distribution and shape of the relationship: where it holds, where it bends, and where it breaks.

Key Insights

  • There is a clear positive relationship between GDP per capita and HDI, but it is not linear and not mechanical. Income creates possibilities — it does not guarantee outcomes.
  • Sub-Saharan Africa shows a visible cluster of countries with both low GDP and low HDI, but also a few emerging movers that are improving on one or both dimensions.
  • Some countries, such as Cabo Verde or Bhutan–type profiles, achieve relatively strong HDI scores despite modest income levels. This suggests effective investment in health, education, and social infrastructure.

These patterns matter because they move the conversation beyond “rich vs poor” and towards how well countries convert economic resources into human wellbeing.

Why This Matters

For policymakers, donors, and analysts, this kind of work supports more nuanced benchmarking: not just “who is rich?” but “who is doing the most with what they have?”.

From a technical point of view, the project demonstrates how Python can be used to: structure messy development data, create interpretable visuals, and guide deeper questions rather than just produce a single summary statistic.
